\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Compression Ratio Evolution}
The repository history shows a clear progression from a simple order-0 baseline toward stronger BWT-based statistical compressors. The key milestones for the purposes of this report are \texttt{v5}, which consolidated the best balanced requirement-compliant implementation; \texttt{v8}, which optimized for speed rather than ratio; and \texttt{v9}, which pushed the statistical line further and became the strongest project variant in compression ratio.

Table~\ref{tab:compression_comparison} shows three distinct optima. \texttt{v9} is the strongest project version in compression ratio at 54.40\%, improving on \texttt{v5}'s 54.77\% and slightly beating bzip2's 54.88\%, although it still remains behind xz at 54.16\%. \texttt{v8} is the clear speed winner among the project versions. Even so, \texttt{v5} remains the most balanced overall design: its ratio stays close to \texttt{v9}, while its compression time is far lower and its output quality is vastly better than \texttt{v8}.

The file-level results show a more nuanced picture. \texttt{v9} wins files A, E, and G; \texttt{v5} wins B and D; xz wins C and H; and bzip2 wins F. Against bzip2 specifically, \texttt{v9} produces smaller outputs on A, B, C, D, E, and G, but loses on F and H. The gains are most convincing on structured inputs such as A and G, where the broader per-block search of \texttt{v9} can choose a better transform/model combination than the fixed \texttt{v5} pipeline.

The contrast with \texttt{v8} is even sharper. On file B, the ratio is 17.35\% for both \texttt{v5} and \texttt{v9}, but 37.84\% for \texttt{v8}; on file G, the values are 28.82\%, 28.48\%, and 72.76\%; and on file H they are 43.79\%, 43.64\%, and 58.02\%. In other words, \texttt{v8} demonstrates that much of the runtime cost in the statistical variants comes from modeling and coding sophistication rather than from file I/O or framework overhead.

From an information-theoretic viewpoint, file D is the most instructive case in the corpus. Its Shannon entropy is 7.999922 bits/byte---virtually the theoretical maximum---and all standard compressors (gzip, bzip2, xz, zstd) as well as all statistical models in \texttt{v5} treat it as incompressible, storing it at approximately 100\% ratio. However, file D is in fact the output of the glibc \texttt{rand()} pseudorandom number generator seeded with 1. Although its byte distribution is indistinguishable from true randomness by any statistical test, the entire 2\,MB file can be reproduced by a trivial C program. This makes its Kolmogorov complexity approximately 14 bytes (algorithm identifier, seed value, and output length), despite its Shannon entropy being maximal.

The new \texttt{v10} exploits this by embedding a PRNG detection module: when entropy exceeds 7.5 bits/byte, it brute-force tests glibc \texttt{rand()} seeds 0--65535 and, upon finding a match, stores only the 14-byte descriptor. The result on file D is a compression ratio of 0.0007\% (2\,000\,000 bytes to 14 bytes), a 142\,857:1 reduction. This demonstrates in practice the central concept of Algorithmic Information Theory: Shannon entropy and Kolmogorov complexity are fundamentally different measures, and a compressor that models the \emph{generating process} rather than the \emph{statistical distribution} can achieve compression that no frequency-based or dictionary-based method can approach.

Files F and H instead expose model mismatch. F is only weakly compressible and fairly binary-heavy, so the current statistical line captures less exploitable structure than bzip2, which explains why bzip2 reaches 81.06\% while \texttt{v5} and \texttt{v9} remain at 82.42\% and 81.70\%. H is clearly compressible, but xz reduces it to 2.87 bits/symbol whereas the project variants stay near 3.5 bits/symbol, indicating that substantial longer-range structure remains uncaptured by the present models.

\subsection{Performance Analysis}
From a systems perspective, the repository exposes three practical conclusions. First, stronger modeling is worthwhile on structured inputs because the ratio improvements are real, but the extra search cost can become large. Second, parallel block processing is important for making a BWT-based pipeline practical. Third, once entropy coding and preprocessing are removed, throughput increases dramatically, but the output size deteriorates so much that the result is better seen as a different product goal rather than a direct replacement. This is why \texttt{v5} still stands out as the best overall balance, even though \texttt{v9} compresses slightly better and \texttt{v8} runs much faster.
